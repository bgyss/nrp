The production track on my Neural Render Proxies reimplementation (Sancho et
al., EGSR 2026) is complete: 10 rungs, three summit demos — one each for
games, animated film, and feature VFX — all measured on a single Apple Silicon
laptop, no CUDA, every claim backed by a committed report.

What shipped:

🎮 Games: the real trained kitchen-scene proxy (409k params, hashgrid ported
to WGSL) relit live in real Chrome with production controls — 481 frames under
interaction at 512², p95 30.7 ms (the 33 ms bar holds), 12/12 sampled frames
pass the perceptual quality gate. Getting the shader under the 30 fps floor
took a ~10× optimization pass; the failed variants are documented too.

🎬 Film: a 120-frame final-tier shot where every frame passes a 40 dB gate via
proxy + fp16 residual reconstruction — 118× faster than re-rendering each
frame. The honest part: the residuals cost 1.17× the raw frames in storage.
The win is time, not bytes, and the report says so.

💡 VFX: an 8-light rig with per-light proxies, solo/mute, and an inverse
art-direction loop that recovers hand-authored light colors by gradient
descent.

That last one is the result I'd most want other ML practitioners to read. The
image-space convergence metric said 154.5 dB — essentially perfect. Digging
into per-light gradients showed only 3 of 6 colorable lights were actually
recovered. The other 3 had proxies that output *exactly zero* — zero output,
zero gradient, so the optimizer "converged" by never touching them, and the
composite couldn't show it because a zero-output light contributes nothing
either way. A perfect score that was half vacuous.

This week's audit narrowed the mystery: the training targets for those lights
are measurably nonzero, so the collapse happens *inside training* — all three
dead proxies are quad lights, all three healthy ones are spheres, and the same
quad code trains fine on simpler scenes. Leading suspect: a zero-collapse
basin in the paper's relative-MSE loss under mostly-dark sampled targets.
Diagnosing it for real is rung 1 of the next phase — a hardening track that
fixes the surfaced failures, re-earns the two caveated demo claims, and then
re-issues the feasibility verdict.

Takeaway I keep re-learning: aggregate metrics can't tell you *which parts* of
a system earned the score. Instrument the parts.

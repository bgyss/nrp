Wrapped up a 10-item roadmap on my Neural Render Proxies reimplementation — a small,
CPU/laptop-GPU-runnable replica of Sancho et al.'s EGSR 2026 paper on
differentiable, interactive relighting via path-cache-compressing MLPs.

Highlights from this cycle, all measured on a single Apple Silicon laptop (no
CUDA), every number backed by a committed report:

📈 Paper-scale training took the quality ceiling from 25.9 dB → 35.2 dB held-out
PSNR (8×256 network, multiresolution hash encoding, 50k iterations) — with
bit-exact checkpoint/resume so long runs are safe to interrupt.

🔬 Ran the paper's data-efficiency claim honestly and it didn't hold at toy scale —
a fixed image dataset beat the rolling training pool by ~1.8 dB at matched budget.
Traced it to pool-size-limited light diversity rather than papering over the
discrepancy. Negative results are still results.

🧩 Multi-view and per-layer compositing NRPs: three camera views compress to 0.76 MB
of resident proxies (vs 10.5 MB of path caches they replace), and per-layer proxies
actually *beat* the full-scene proxy on their own pixels by 3.5-4.7 dB.

⚡ Batched GPU GATHERLIGHT hits 5-7x speedups on Apple's MPS backend at scale, and a
packed fp16 + shared-exponent cache format shrinks storage 3-4x with under 0.1 dB
quality cost.

📊 Added SSIM/FLIP perceptual metrics to the ablation suite — and they caught
something PSNR alone missed: a bare-bones model with no auxiliary features looked
almost competitive on PSNR while producing a visibly wrong, low-frequency lighting
wash.

144 unit tests green, every claim traced to a script and a JSON report. The fun part
of reimplementing a paper isn't just getting the numbers to look right — it's being
honest about the one place they don't.

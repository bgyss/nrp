Closed out a six-part "scale and speed" pass on my Neural Render Proxies
reimplementation (Sancho et al., CGF 45(4), EGSR 2026) — the goal was
simple to state and harder to earn: take a pipeline proven correct at toy
resolution and push it to real scene sizes, real render resolutions, and a
real browser runtime, without quietly lowering the bar to get there.

**Streamed training, 6.04x.** The gather step's inner loop was Python-bound.
Vectorized it — no change to what's being computed, just how.

**Two real scenes at real resolution.** 1024² renders at 23.0 dB PSNR on
the kitchen scene, plus a second full scene (bedroom, 21.89 dB) — moving
past single-scene, toy-resolution proofs of concept.

**Export/save, 5.78x-9.13x faster at scale**, with a separate 1.87x on the
exporter's bincount path — the plumbing that makes larger scenes practical
to actually iterate on.

**Re-earned the rig-additivity gate.** Rebuilt it under kernel conditioning
and, instead of reporting one aggregate pass/fail number, decomposed the
result per light — an honest additivity failure broken down by light
rather than smoothed into a single score.

**A real bug, found before trusting a number.** The WebGPU benchmark
harness was JSON-encoding G-buffer and reference data as plain JS number
arrays; at 1024² that's 9.4M+3.1M floats, and the payload crashed the
target page before any 1024² result could be trusted. Fixed by
base64-encoding the blobs and decoding client-side. Once fixed: WebGPU
still beats MPS full-frame inference at every resolution tested, it just
isn't real-time yet at 1024² (11.3 fps p95 against a 30 fps floor).

**A clean triple negative, reported straight.** Ran the throughput sweep
everyone eventually runs — bigger batch, AMP/fp16/bf16 autocast,
torch.compile — expecting at least one lever to help on this hardware.
None did: bigger batch loses PSNR faster than it gains iters/s; autocast
is a wash on MPS and 25-29x *slower* on CPU (no native low-precision GEMM
path on this Apple Silicon backend); torch.compile crashes outright on MPS
and nets a small loss on CPU once compile overhead is counted. Included a
stalled-looking sweep that turned out to be a genuine slow result, not a
hang, once swap-thrashing was ruled out. None of this is a setback — it's
the real baseline for what needs re-measuring once this moves to a rented
CUDA GPU, where none of the three should be assumed to "just work" either.

Everything above still runs CPU-only, no GPU required. Full numbers and
code in the repo — link in comments.

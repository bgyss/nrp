Last week I posted about a "perfect" 154.5 dB convergence result on my Neural
Render Proxies reimplementation (Sancho et al., EGSR 2026) that turned out to
be half fake: 3 of 8 trained light proxies were outputting exactly zero, so
an inverse-optimization loop "converged" on them by never touching them, and
the composite-level metric couldn't see it because a zero-output light
contributes nothing to the image either way. I knew the targets those
proxies were supposed to learn were nonzero. I didn't know why training
still drove them to exactly zero. This week: the answer, and a one-line fix.

**The mechanism.** The output head is a softplus, and its default
initialization predicts ~0.69 regardless of scene. On this cache the true
target brightness is ~130–700× dimmer than that. So for most training steps
the loss gradient points the same direction: down. Adam's per-step move
turns out to be roughly a fixed size regardless of how large that gradient
actually is — I confirmed this by turning the gradient's scale up 100× and
by clipping it outright; neither changed the trajectory at all. With a
persistent one-directional push of constant size, the network's pre-output
value marches toward negative infinity at a steady rate. Once it crosses the
point where softplus's derivative underflows to exact zero in float32, the
gradient signal is gone — permanently. That threshold gets crossed within
the first ~50 of 800 training iterations.

**The twist:** this isn't a quad-light-specific bug. The healthiest-looking
proxy in last week's rig — a working sphere light — shows the *identical*
drift, just slower, which is exactly why it was already the worst-scoring
light in the rig before I knew any of this.

**The fix:** initialize the output layer's bias to match the true target
scale instead of the default. One method, called once, at negligible cost
(0.14 seconds against a ~5-minute training run). Verified on the real cache:
all 3 previously-dead proxies now produce nonzero output at their correct
light positions, with quality now in the same range as the working spheres
— the systematic gap between light types is gone.

One more thing the audit surfaced, worth sitting with: before the fix, the
three broken proxies scored *higher* on validation PSNR than the working
sphere did. A network that always predicts zero looks like a great model
when most of your validation targets are dark. The aggregate metric wasn't
just blind to the bug — it rewarded it.

Diagnosing this the right way meant not fixing anything until an ablation
matrix ruled out every wrong hypothesis first: the loss's epsilon term,
gradient clipping, and the light-sampling scheme I originally suspected all
turned out to be red herrings. The actual lever was learning-rate-scale
sensitivity in how Adam interacts with a saturating output activation — an
architecture property, not a data property.

This is rung 1 of a new hardening track that goes back through the
production track's caveats and negative results: re-earning last week's
convergence claim with all 8 lights genuinely live, porting the light-rig
compositing to the real-time WebGPU runtime, and re-issuing the project's
feasibility verdict against the full evidence base. More as it lands.
